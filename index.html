Challenge 1: Students Don't Understand Why to Experiment with Predictions

The issue: "Why does it matter what AI predicts if I'm just going to write what I want anyway?"

Immediate response: "Great question! Two reasons: (1) Understanding what's PROBABLE helps you decide when to surprise your reader. If you know what they expect, you can either deliver it satisfyingly OR subvert it interestingly. (2) AI's predictions reveal patterns and clichÃ©sâ€”what's been done before. Sometimes you'll think 'That's exactly right!' but other times you'll think 'That's too obvious'â€”and both reactions are valuable creative information."

For next time: Include a mini-example in openingâ€”show a familiar story scenario and ask what students predict, then reveal what actually happens and discuss why it works.

Challenge 2: AI Predictions Are Too Generic or Stereotypical

The issue: Multiple students report that AI's predictions feel boring, clichÃ©d, or stereotypical.

Immediate response: "This is a valuable discovery! WHY do they feel generic? Connect to pattern recognitionâ€”AI is showing you what's statistically common in similar stories. That's exactly why YOUR creative judgment matters. AI can tell you what's typical, but only YOU can decide what's right for your story. Use the boring prediction as a signal: 'If this is the obvious choice, what would be unexpected but make sense?'"

Teaching moment: This is actually evidence of students' growing sophistication. They're recognizing when probability-based predictions lack creative nuance. Celebrate this critical thinking.

Challenge 3: Students Over-Rely on AI Suggestions

The issue: Some students accept AI's first prediction without evaluation or experimentation.

Immediate response: Ask probing questions: "Is this what AI predicts or what YOU want? Try asking for three different optionsâ€”which one feels most alive to you? What makes it better than the others?" Emphasize: The goal is developing YOUR editorial judgment, not finding AI's answers.

For next time: Build in requirement: "Try at least TWO different prediction prompts before making your creative choice." Structure forces experimentation.

Challenge 4: Students Can't Articulate Why a Prediction Works/Doesn't Work

The issue: Students know they like/dislike a prediction but can't explain why using probability concepts.

Immediate response: Provide thinking prompts:

  • "Does it surprise you? Good surprise or bad surprise?"
  • "Would a reader see this coming?"
  • "Does it fit your character's logic or story's logic?"
  • "Is it what you've seen before or something fresh?"
  • "If you told a friend this plot point, would they say 'interesting!' or 'that's obvious'?"

For next time: Create a simple evaluation rubric students can reference: Predictability (high/medium/low), Fit with story logic (yes/no), Freshness (generic/fresh/in-between), Reader surprise (none/mild/strong).

Challenge 5: Discussion Feels Forced or Unproductive

The issue: If you include the optional discussion, students don't engage meaningfully.

Immediate response: Skip it. Say: "I can tell you're all deep in your creative workâ€”let's protect that time. Keep working, and your field journal reflection will capture your probability thinking."

Remember: Discussion is optional for a reason. If students are genuinely engaged in creative experimentation, discussion interrupts rather than enhances learning. Trust the field journal to capture their insights.

Challenge 6: Technical Issues with Alia-Creative

Immediate response: Have backup activity ready: Students can manually explore probability without AI. "Brainstorm three different plot developments for this scene: one high-probability (expected), one low-probability (surprising), one magic-zone (unexpected but fits). Which would you choose? Why?" This still develops probability thinking without requiring AI access.
